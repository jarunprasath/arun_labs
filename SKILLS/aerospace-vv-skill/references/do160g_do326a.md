# DO-160G & DO-326A Deep Reference — Environmental Testing & Airworthiness Security

---

## PART 1: DO-160G — Environmental Conditions and Test Procedures for Airborne Equipment

**Standard:** RTCA DO-160G / EUROCAE ED-14G  
**Date:** December 8, 2010  
**Prepared by:** SC-135 (RTCA)  
**Supersedes:** DO-160F  
**Purpose:** Define minimum standard environmental test conditions (categories) and applicable test procedures for airborne equipment

---

## 1. PURPOSE AND APPLICABILITY (Section 1)

DO-160G defines a series of minimum standard environmental test conditions (categories) and applicable test procedures for airborne equipment. Purpose: provide a laboratory means of determining performance characteristics of airborne equipment in environmental conditions representative of those encountered in airborne operation.

Tests can be used with applicable equipment performance standards as a minimum specification under environmental conditions providing sufficient degree of confidence in performance during operations.

**What DO-160G does NOT do:**
- Does not determine service life of airborne equipment subjected to tests
- Some environmental conditions not included (hail, acceleration, acoustic vibration are NOT covered)
- Does not include all possible environmental conditions — special environmental conditions uniquely related to specific equipment are the responsibility of the equipment specification writer

**How DO-160G fits in the certification package:**
- Equipment suppliers select categories best representing the most severe environment regularly expected
- Selected categories tabulated on the Environmental Qualification Form (Appendix A)
- For any category listed: equipment would also be able to perform intended functions in LESS SEVERE categories
- If categories on the Environmental Qualification Form are insufficient for a specific certification: additional testing may be required
- Regulatory application is the sole responsibility of governmental (regulatory) agencies

**Historical note**: DO-160 has been used for environmental qualification testing since 1958 (precursor DO-138). Referenced in FAA Advisory Circulars as means of environmental qualification for TSO authorization.

---

## 2. DEFINITIONS (Section 2)

**Equipment Temperature Stabilization:**
- Not Operating: temperature of largest internal mass within 3°C of specified test temperature; minimum 3 hours if temperature measurement not practical
- Operating: functioning parts not varying more than 2°C per hour; minimum 2 hours if measurement not practical

**Maximum Duty Cycle**: Relationship between maximum length of operation at rated capacity and length of time not operating (or at defined minimum capacity). Established by equipment specification.

**Not Operating**: No power applied to equipment unless otherwise defined by individual equipment specification.

**Controlled or Partially Controlled Temperature Location**: Space within aircraft where temperature maintained by environmental control system (see Table 4-1 for applicable categories).

**Total Excursion**: Total displacement from positive maximum to negative maximum.

**Equipment**: Test items and all components necessary for the equipment to properly perform its intended function(s). Must be representative of production standard to be used in service.

**Altitude**: Environmental pressure relative to sea level to which equipment exposed during tests.

**Category of Tests**: Each test section provides categories representing different severity levels. Use Category X when compliance with applicable equipment performance standards has NOT been demonstrated under that environmental condition.

---

## 3. CONDITIONS OF TESTS (Section 3)

### Test Setup Rules
- **Connection and Orientation (§3.1)**: Equipment connected and oriented as closely as possible to the manner it will be used in aircraft installation
- **Order of Tests (§3.2)**: Multiple test articles permissible (subject to §3.2 provisions); order of tests not specified unless noted in individual section
- **Combining Tests (§3.3)**: May combine environmental conditions where specified or permitted
- **Air Temperature in Test Chamber (§3.4)**: Measured near the equipment under test (EUT); location per applicable section procedures
- **Ambient Conditions (§3.5)**: Temperature +15 to +35°C, relative humidity not greater than 85%, altitude approximately sea level (80-106 kPa)
- **Environmental Test Condition Tolerances (§3.6)**: Specified in each section; unless otherwise specified: temperature ±2°C, relative humidity ±5%
- **EUT Configuration for Susceptibility Tests (§3.9)**: Operating in its normal mode during susceptibility tests

### Section 5 — Temperature Variation

**Purpose**: Tests equipment ability to withstand and operate through rapid temperature changes (thermal shock).

**Categories:**
- **Category A**: Equipment in controlled locations — rate of temperature change not exceeding 2°C per minute
- **Category B**: Equipment in partially controlled locations — rate not exceeding 5°C per minute
- **Category C**: Equipment in uncontrolled locations — rapid change (shock): equipment moved between extreme temperatures

**Test procedure**: Equipment cycled between specified low and high temperatures at specified rates; must operate satisfactorily throughout.

**Why this matters**: Thermal expansion and contraction, solder joint fatigue, seal integrity, connector reliability.

### Section 6 — Humidity

**Purpose**: Determines ability of equipment to withstand effects of high humidity including condensation.

**Categories:**
- **Category A**: Controlled environments — standard humidity test (warm/humid cycle 95% RH, moderate temperatures)
- **Category B**: Uncontrolled environments with condensation possible — severe humidity cycle (65°C, 95% RH)
- **Category C**: Severe humidity with temperature cycling

**Test procedure**: Equipment placed in humidity chamber; subjected to combinations of temperature and humidity (typically 95% RH); equipment operated during and after test to verify performance.

**Failure modes tested**: Corrosion, insulation resistance degradation, connector contamination, moisture absorption.

### Section 7 — Operational Shocks and Crash Safety

**Purpose**: Tests equipment ability to withstand mechanical shocks occurring during normal operations (door slams, hard landings, taxiing) and to maintain structural integrity during crash conditions.

**Categories (Operational Shocks):**
- **Category A**: Standard shock (6g, 11ms half-sine pulse) — standard for most avionic installations
- **Category B**: Severe shock (20g, 11ms half-sine)

**Crash Safety:** All categories include crash safety test (20g, 11ms half-sine — representative of survivable crash loads) to confirm equipment does not detach and injure occupants or cause secondary hazards.

**Test procedure**: Equipment (not operating during crash safety) subjected to shock pulses in three axes; visual inspection and performance check after.

### Section 8 — Vibration

**Purpose**: Determines ability of equipment to withstand and operate in vibration environments found in aircraft.

Three vibration test types in DO-160G:
1. **Sinusoidal Vibration**: Sweep through frequency range at specified levels
2. **Random Vibration**: Broadband random vibration at specified power spectral density
3. **Vibration and Shock**: Combined environment

**Categories** (defined by vibration curve — frequency vs displacement/acceleration):
- **Category S**: Sinusoidal only (older, less representative)
- **Category R**: Random vibration using curves (more modern, more realistic)
- **Category U**: Random vibration with specific curves for helicopter and jet installations
- **Category H**: Helicopter-specific vibration
- **Category M**: Fixed-wing turbine aircraft
- **Category P, Q, R, S**: Severity levels within random vibration categories
- **Category Z**: Custom profile (equipment supplier defined)

**Why matters for avionics:** Different installations have radically different vibration environments. Equipment in engine nacelles experiences far more severe vibration than equipment in pressurized equipment bays. Category must match installation location.

**Typical avionics equipment bay:** Category [R or S][number] depending on aircraft type and location.

### Section 9 — Explosion Proofness

**Purpose**: Tests that equipment does not ignite flammable fuel-air mixtures — relevant for equipment installed near fuel tanks, fuel vents, or in zones with potential explosive atmosphere.

**Categories:**
- **Category E**: Equipment is explosion-proof (has been tested; suitable for installation in potentially explosive zones)
- **Category X**: No testing performed; must NOT be installed in zones with explosive atmosphere

**Test procedure**: Equipment operated in explosive atmosphere (specified fuel-air mixture concentration) while subjected to operational stress; no ignition must occur.

**Key safety implication**: Critical for fuel management computers, pressure sensors near tanks, equipment in fuel bay zones.

### Section 10 — Waterproofness

**Purpose**: Determines equipment's ability to withstand exposure to water — dripping, splashing, or spray.

**Categories:**
- **Category W**: Waterproofness to dripping water (vertical installation, limited water exposure)
- **Category R**: Water resistance to rain and splash
- **Category S**: Waterproof to direct water spray
- **Category X**: No waterproofness testing; should not be exposed to water

**Test procedure**: Water applied at specified rate and angle; equipment operated during and after; must meet applicable performance standards.

**When applicable**: External sensors, flight deck equipment (cockpit window spray), landing gear zone equipment.

### Section 11 — Fluids Susceptibility

**Purpose**: Tests equipment's resistance to the effects of fluids likely to be encountered in normal operation (hydraulic fluid, fuel, lubricating oil, cleaning solvents, deicing fluid).

**Categories:**
- **Category F**: Equipment exposed to fluids specified in applicable standard; tested by immersion or spray
- **Category X**: Not tested; should not be exposed to specific fluids

### Section 12 — Sand and Dust

**Purpose**: Tests performance and structural integrity when exposed to sand and dust particles.

**Categories:**
- **Category D**: Blowing dust test (coarse dust at 200 mg/m³ concentration, 10 m/s wind)
- **Category S**: Sand test (larger particles at higher concentration)

### Section 13 — Fungus Resistance

**Purpose**: Tests that equipment materials do not support fungus growth which could damage insulation or cause contamination.

**Category F**: Inoculated with spore suspensions and incubated; visual examination for fungus growth.

### Section 14 — Salt Spray

**Purpose**: Tests corrosion resistance when exposed to salt spray (seawater atmosphere).

**Category S**: Equipment exposed to salt fog at specified temperature and duration; visual inspection and performance check.

**When applicable**: Equipment installed on aircraft operating in maritime environments; external components; connectors.

### Section 15 — Magnetic Effect

**Purpose**: Tests for magnetic fields generated by equipment that could deflect aircraft magnetic compass.

**Category Z**: Equipment placed at specified distance from compass-equivalent; angular deflection of compass measured; must not exceed specified limits.

### Section 16 — Power Input

**Purpose**: Tests equipment performance when powered by AC or DC power with characteristics representative of aircraft electrical systems — including voltage variations, frequency variations, emergency conditions.

**Key test conditions:**
- Normal operation voltage range
- Emergency low voltage
- High voltage transients
- Power interruptions (specified duration)
- Frequency variations (AC systems)
- Voltage ripple

**Categories (DC systems):**
- **Category A**: 28 VDC systems — normal range ±10%; emergency conditions specified
- **Category B**: 14 VDC systems
- **Category Z**: Custom power input requirements (e.g., 270 VDC high-voltage systems)

**Categories (AC systems):**
- Frequency: 360–800 Hz (400 Hz nominal)
- Voltage: 108–118 VAC (115 VAC nominal), 216–236 VAC (230 VAC nominal)
- Phase balance requirements

**Why critical**: Aircraft electrical systems have dynamic characteristics unlike commercial power. Equipment must survive bus transients, switchovers, and emergency battery conditions while continuing to operate.

### Section 17 — Voltage Spike

**Purpose**: Tests equipment immunity to transient overvoltage spikes on DC power buses — caused by switching loads, arc events, and other transients on aircraft power systems.

**Categories:**
- **Category A**: 900V spike, 10 µs rise time, on 28 VDC systems
- **Category B**: 1800V spike (higher severity)
- **Category H**: High-voltage spikes for systems with higher bus voltages
- **Category Z**: Custom spike specifications

**Test procedure**: Equipment operating; spike of specified waveform and amplitude injected on power leads; equipment must continue operating or recover to normal operation.

**Distinction from Section 22 (Lightning)**: Section 17 addresses CONDUCTION on power bus (not induced by lightning); Section 22 addresses lightning-induced transients on signal and power wiring.

### Section 18 — Audio Frequency Conducted Susceptibility — Power Inputs

**Purpose**: Tests immunity to audio-frequency (power line) interference conducted via power inputs. Addresses equipment sensitivity to harmonic content on power bus from other equipment (motors, switching power supplies, etc.).

**Test procedure**: Modulated signals superimposed on power input; equipment must operate within specification.

**Categories reflect** frequency range and amplitude of interference expected in specific installation environments.

### Section 19 — Induced Signal Susceptibility

**Purpose**: Tests immunity to low-frequency interference signals induced in cabling and connector pins from adjacent wiring, power cables, or cross-talk between signal paths.

**Categories:**
- **Category A through L**: Different combinations of voltage levels and frequency ranges
- Covers both common-mode and differential-mode interference

**Test procedure**: Test signals injected into signal pins via wire harness or direct injection; equipment must maintain performance.

**Relationship to Section 22**: Section 19 covers general induced signals; Section 22 covers specifically lightning-induced transients.

### Section 20 — Radio Frequency Susceptibility (Radiated and Conducted)

**Purpose**: Tests immunity to radiofrequency electromagnetic fields and conducted RF interference. Covers susceptibility from transmitters, radar, airport navigation equipment, portable electronic devices.

**Two components:**
1. **Radiated RF Susceptibility**: Equipment exposed to electromagnetic field strengths in antenna-free zone of test facility; specified field strengths (V/m) at specified frequencies
2. **Conducted RF Susceptibility**: RF signals injected directly onto power and signal lines

**Categories:**
- **Category T**: Lower level RF susceptibility (general electronics bays)
- **Category W**: Higher level (locations with more RF exposure)
- **Category R, S, L, M**: Frequency-specific and level-specific categories

**Frequency ranges covered**: Typically 10 kHz to 18 GHz (radiated); 150 kHz to 400 MHz (conducted)

**Why critical**: Aircraft have many transmitters (VHF, HF, SATCOM, TCAS, radar transponder, DME) and the equipment must work in presence of all of them. Also must withstand strong field environments at airports.

### Section 21 — Emission of Radio Frequency Energy

**Purpose**: Tests that equipment does not emit electromagnetic energy that could interfere with aircraft navigation or communication systems. Covers CONDUCTED and RADIATED emissions.

**Two components:**
1. **Conducted Emissions**: RF energy conducted back onto power and signal buses; must not exceed specified levels
2. **Radiated Emissions**: RF energy radiated from equipment chassis and wiring; must not exceed specified levels

**Categories:**
- **Category A**: Standard for most commercial avionics
- **Category B through M**: Varying frequency ranges and emission limits

**Frequency ranges**: Typically 150 kHz to 6 GHz

**Why critical for Honeywell EGPWS/Weather Radar context**: Weather radar is a high-power transmitter. The radar system's emissions are carefully controlled to avoid interfering with VHF comm, ILS, and other navigation systems. Section 21 compliance demonstrations are a key part of radar TSO certification.

### Section 22 — Lightning Induced Transient Susceptibility

**Purpose**: Tests equipment immunity to transient currents and voltages INDUCED in aircraft wiring and on connector pins by lightning strikes to the aircraft. Covers both direct power pins and signal pins.

**Background**: When lightning strikes the aircraft, transient currents flow through the airframe and induce currents/voltages in the internal wiring harnesses. These induced transients can damage or upset electronic equipment.

**Two injection methods:**
1. **Pin Injection (§22.5.1)**: Transients injected directly into equipment connector pins — simulates induced currents in short wiring runs
2. **Cable Bundle Injection (§22.5.2)**: Transient current transformer wrapped around cable bundle — simulates induced currents in longer wiring runs

**Test waveforms (per DO-160G §22.5.3):**
- **Waveform Set 1 (Single Stroke)**: Represents first return stroke of negative lightning flash; highest peak current; 6.4/69 µs waveform
- **Waveform Set 2 (Multiple Stroke)**: Represents subsequent return strokes; 6.4/69 µs repeated
- **Waveform Set 3 (Multiple Burst)**: Long-continuing current with superimposed bursts

**Severity Levels (§22.5.3):** 
Levels 1 through 5, with Level 5 being most severe. Equipment certified for external/exposed zones uses Level 3-5; equipment in shielded equipment bays typically Level 1-2.

**Performance Categories (§22.5.4):**
After each test, equipment must meet applicable performance standards per the test's "performance category":
- **Category A**: No degradation of function or performance during and after test
- **Category B**: Degradation of function or performance during test but self-recovery to normal after test
- **Category C**: Temporary degradation requiring intervention (e.g., restart/reset) to recover; no physical damage
- **Category D**: Temporary degradation acceptable if annunciated, permanent impairment acceptable within defined limits (used for non-critical functions only)
- **Category E**: Physical damage acceptable — must not cause hazard

**Equipment zones on aircraft** determine required severity levels:
- Internal equipment bays (fully shielded): Level 1
- Partially shielded locations: Level 2-3
- External locations/poor shielding: Level 4-5

**Why critical for EGPWS/radar (Arun's Honeywell context)**: EGPWS external antennas (GPS, radio altimeter) and weather radar antenna feedlines are EXTERNAL connections. Lightning-induced transients on these lines must be managed. Section 22 testing with appropriate pin injection levels is required for antenna/feedline connections. Weather radar waveguides may be subject to Section 22 testing at the equipment input port.

### Section 23 — Lightning Direct Effects

**Purpose**: Tests equipment or equipment installations for resistance to DIRECT LIGHTNING ATTACHMENT and the resulting structural and thermal effects. This differs from Section 22 which addresses induced effects.

**Applicability**: Primarily for structural components, radomes, antennas, and externally mounted equipment that could receive direct lightning attachment.

**Test levels correspond to lightning current zones on aircraft:**
- Zone 1A: Direct attachment expected (wingtips, radomes, fuselage extremities)
- Zone 2A: Swept stroke attachment (fuselage sides, nacelles)
- Zone 3: Conduction only (interior areas)

**Tests may include:**
- High current arc attachment to metal structures or antenna masts
- Thermal effects on composite materials (delamination, burn-through)
- Structural integrity under magnetic force loads

**Note for interview context**: Section 23 is more relevant to aircraft structures and antenna design than to internal LRU electronics (that's Section 22). However, for Arun's EGPWS context: EGPWS GPS antenna if mounted on aircraft exterior is subject to both Section 22 (induced) and potentially Section 23 (direct) assessment.

### Section 24 — Icing

**Purpose**: Tests equipment performance when ice forms on the equipment — relevant for external equipment or equipment in cold areas prone to condensation and freezing.

**Categories:**
- **Category A**: Equipment that could be exposed to ice — ice crystal tests
- **Category B**: Freezing rain

**Test procedure**: Equipment exposed to specified icing conditions; must operate during and after icing.

**When applicable**: External sensors (pitot probes, angle-of-attack vanes), cockpit displays exposed to window frosting, equipment in unpressurized/unheated compartments.

### Section 25 — Electrostatic Discharge (ESD)

**Purpose**: Tests equipment immunity to ESD events resulting from personnel touching equipment during maintenance or operation.

**Test levels (based on IEC 61000-4-2):**
- **Level 1**: 2 kV contact / 4 kV air discharge (sensitive locations, protected environments)
- **Level 2**: 4 kV contact / 8 kV air discharge (normal maintenance environments)
- **Level 3**: 6 kV contact / 12 kV air discharge (challenging environments)
- **Level 4**: 8 kV contact / 15 kV air discharge (high severity)

**Categories A-D**: Defined by whether equipment is tested to Level 2, 3, 4, or with specific modifications.

**Test procedure**: ESD gun applied to accessible metal surfaces and corners of equipment while operating; performance check after.

**Why matters**: Avionics equipment is routinely handled during maintenance; ESD events from personnel can damage CMOS circuits or cause upset in microcontrollers. Section 25 compliance demonstrates immunity.

### Section 26 — Fire, Flammability

**Purpose**: Tests that equipment materials do not propagate fire and meet flammability requirements for installation in aircraft.

**Test types:**
- **Horizontal Burn Test**: Material strip burned horizontally; burn rate must not exceed specified limit
- **Vertical Burn Test**: Material strip burned vertically; must self-extinguish within specified time
- **Smoke Density**: Materials must not produce excessive smoke
- **Toxicity**: Combustion products must not exceed specified toxicity limits

**Categories:** Multiple categories depending on aircraft zone, airworthiness requirements, and type of material.

**Key regulatory link**: Section 26 DO-160G test results are required evidence for FAA/EASA approval of materials used in aircraft interiors and systems under CS-25 §853 (fire protection) and related regulations.

## 5. ENVIRONMENTAL QUALIFICATION FORM (Appendix A)

The Environmental Qualification Form (EQF) is a table identifying which tests were performed and which categories were demonstrated. The EQF is a required output of DO-160G testing and becomes part of the certification evidence package.

EQF contents: equipment name/part number/serial number, section number for each DO-160G test, applicable category tested, any special conditions, date and test facility.

If a section shows "X" → equipment has NOT been demonstrated to comply with that environmental condition and must NOT be installed in locations subject to that condition.

## 6. HOW DO-160G FITS IN THE CERTIFICATION PACKAGE

```
Aircraft Level (CS-25 / FAR Part 25)
    └── System Level (ARP4754A — FDAL/IDAL assignment, FHA/PSSA/SSA)
            └── Item Level — LRU (Line Replaceable Unit)
                    ├── Software: DO-178C (SAS → DER approval)
                    ├── Hardware: DO-254 (HAS → DER approval) [if FPGA/ASIC]
                    └── Environmental: DO-160G (Qualification Test Report + EQF)
```

The DO-160G Qualification Test Report and EQF become part of the equipment Technical Standard Order (TSO) authorization application or STC/TC certification evidence. TSO authorization (ETSO in Europe) typically references DO-160G as the environmental test standard.

**Key certification pathway:**
- Equipment manufacturer selects DO-160G categories based on installation location analysis
- Tests performed at accredited test laboratory
- Qualification Test Report (QTR) produced documenting test setups, results, pass/fail
- EQF completed and included in TSO/certification data package
- DER/EASA expert reviews QTR and EQF to confirm qualification

## 7. DO-160G INTERVIEW Q&A

**Q1: What is the difference between DO-160G Section 22 and Section 23?**
A: Section 22 covers LIGHTNING INDUCED TRANSIENT SUSCEPTIBILITY — the immunity of equipment to transient electrical currents and voltages INDUCED into aircraft wiring by a nearby lightning strike. The test methods (pin injection, cable bundle injection) simulate the electromagnetic coupling effects. Section 23 covers LIGHTNING DIRECT EFFECTS — the structural and thermal effects of a lightning bolt DIRECTLY ATTACHING to the equipment or its installation. Section 22 is relevant to almost all avionics (electronic immunity). Section 23 is relevant to structurally exposed components (antennas, radomes, pylons) that could receive a direct strike.

**Q2: Explain the DO-160G category letter system for Section 4 (Temperature and Altitude).**
A: Each category in Section 4 is defined by: (a) ground survival low temperature, (b) short-time operating low temperature, (c) operating low temperature, (d) short-time operating high temperature, (e) operating high temperature, (f) ground survival high temperature, and the maximum altitude. Categories A through F correspond to increasing altitude ceilings: A=15,000 ft, B=25,000 ft, C=35,000 ft, D=50,000 ft, E=70,000 ft, and F for even higher. The number suffix (1, 2, 3, 4) indicates the temperature range and installation type within that altitude band — controlled/partially controlled location, pressurized vs unpressurized.

Equipment in a pressurized avionics bay of a transport aircraft typically uses Category C or D depending on operating altitude (Concorde-era equipment used E categories). Category X is a custom specification defined by the equipment supplier.

**Q3: What performance categories are defined in DO-160G Section 22 and what do they mean?**
A: After lightning induced transient testing (Section 22), equipment must meet applicable performance standards according to its performance category: Category A (no degradation during or after test), Category B (may degrade during test but self-recovers to normal operation after test removal), Category C (temporary degradation requiring intervention to recover — e.g., crew reset — but no physical damage), Category D (temporary degradation acceptable provided it is annunciated and permanent impairment is within defined limits, for non-critical functions only), Category E (physical damage acceptable provided the equipment does not cause a hazard). Higher-criticality functions require Category A or B. Less critical functions might accept Category C. Level-E acceptance would only be for equipment where damage itself is not hazardous.

**Q4: What is the Environmental Qualification Form and why is it required?**
A: The Environmental Qualification Form (EQF, per Appendix A of DO-160G) is a standardized table documenting which DO-160G environmental tests were performed on a specific equipment, which categories were demonstrated, and any special conditions. It is required because: (1) it provides the certification authority with a clear, standardized declaration of the environmental envelope in which the equipment has been demonstrated to perform; (2) it forms the basis for approving specific installation locations — equipment can only be installed in locations whose environmental conditions fall within the demonstrated categories; (3) it is part of the TSO/ETSO application and the certification data package. An entry of "X" for a test section means the equipment was NOT tested for that condition and must not be installed in environments subject to that condition.

**Q5: Why is DO-160G Section 21 particularly important for weather radar systems?**
A: Section 21 covers EMISSION OF RADIO FREQUENCY ENERGY — the measurement of RF energy radiated and conducted by the equipment that could interfere with other aircraft systems. Weather radar systems are high-power transmitters (typically 5–50 kW peak power pulses at C-band or X-band frequencies). The radar antenna sidelobes and any leakage from waveguides or connectors could potentially interfere with VHF communications, ILS/LOC/GS, VOR, and other navigation systems if not properly controlled. Section 21 testing demonstrates that the radar's emissions are below specified limits across the critical frequency ranges, ensuring safe coexistence with other aircraft systems. This is a mandatory part of weather radar TSO (ETSO C63) certification evidence.

## PART 2: DO-326A — Airworthiness Security Process Specification

**Standard:** RTCA DO-326A / EUROCAE ED-202A  
**Date:** 2014  
**Related documents:** DO-356A (Methods and Tools, ED-205), DO-355 (Continuing Airworthiness, ED-204)  
**Regulatory reference:** EASA CS-25 §1319; FAA AC 119-1

---

## 1. PURPOSE AND BACKGROUND

DO-326A defines the **Airworthiness Security Process (ASP)** — the process for identifying and managing cybersecurity threats that could affect the airworthiness of an aircraft.

**Critical distinction**: Airworthiness Security ≠ General IT Security. DO-326A specifically addresses threats whose exploitation could affect SAFE FLIGHT AND LANDING. IT security threats affecting operational efficiency, data privacy, or business continuity are not in scope unless they also have airworthiness implications.

**Why emerging**: Modern aircraft have external connectivity that did not exist historically: ACARS/SATCOM (aircraft-to-ground data), Electronic Flight Bag (EFB) connections, wireless avionics (WAIC — Wireless Avionics Intra-Communication), data loading (ARINC 615/USB/maintenance laptop), airport gate network uplinks, automatic ATIS download. Each creates an attack surface.

**Regulatory anchor**: EASA CS-25 §1319 (incorporated after 2010) requires that aircraft systems be protected from intentional unauthorized electronic interaction that could adversely affect safety. FAA addresses this through AC 119-1 and issue papers on specific programs.

---

## 2. THE DO-326A DOCUMENT FAMILY

The three-document suite:
- **DO-326A / ED-202A**: Airworthiness Security PROCESS — the "what to do" at development and certification level. Defines the ASP, threat condition classification, security risk assessment, and documentation requirements.
- **DO-356A / ED-205**: Methods and Tools for Aviation Cybersecurity — the "how to do it" technical implementation. Specific analysis methods, attack path analysis, tools, technical mitigation approaches.
- **DO-355 / ED-204**: Information Security for Continuing Airworthiness — the "how to maintain it" for in-service aircraft. MRO, airline operational procedures, updates, vulnerability management after type certificate.

Analogy to existing standards: DO-326A is like ARP4754A (process framework). DO-356A is like ARP4761 (methods and tools). DO-355 is like ARP5150 (in-service safety management).

---

## 3. CORE CONCEPTS

### Airworthiness Security Process (ASP)
The structured process to:
1. Identify security threats to aircraft functions with potential airworthiness impact
2. Assess threat conditions (classify by airworthiness effect severity)
3. Define and implement security mitigations
4. Verify mitigations are effective and complete
5. Document compliance for certification authority review
6. Manage in-service security throughout aircraft service life

### Threat Condition
Similar in structure to a Failure Condition in ARP4754A — but caused by INTENTIONAL, UNAUTHORIZED action rather than random failure or development error.

A threat condition is: a combination of a threat (an attacker's capability, intent, and means of access) with a vulnerability (a weakness in the aircraft system that the threat could exploit) resulting in an adverse safety effect.

**Threat condition classification uses same severity levels as ARP4754A failure conditions:**
- Catastrophic → highest assurance security mitigations
- Hazardous/Severe-Major → high assurance security mitigations
- Major → medium assurance security mitigations
- Minor → proportionate response
- No Safety Effect → no mandatory security mitigation required

### Difference from ARP4761 Safety Assessment
| Aspect | ARP4761 Safety Assessment | DO-326A Security Assessment |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------------|
| Threat type | Random failures, equipment failures, environmental events | Intentional, adversary-driven actions |
| Analysis method | FHA, FTA, FMEA (probabilistic) | Threat modeling, Attack Path Analysis (deterministic) |
| Key question | "How often does this fail?" | "Can this be intentionally exploited?" |
| Likelihood basis | Hardware failure rates, MTBF | Attacker capability, motivation, opportunity |
| Common cause | Environmental/manufacturing | Attack campaigns, coordinated intrusions |

**Important**: DO-326A and ARP4761/ARP4754A are COMPLEMENTARY — both are required for modern aircraft certification. A safety argument without security analysis is incomplete for aircraft with external connectivity.

### Security Risk Assessment
The security risk assessment in DO-326A:
- Identifies ATTACK VECTORS (possible paths from external interfaces to safety-critical functions)
- Assesses threat likelihood (attacker capability + motivation + opportunity)
- Assesses consequence severity (using ARP4754A failure condition classification)
- Determines which threats require mitigation vs acceptance
- Unlike safety, intentionality changes the calculation — a sophisticated state-level attacker has different capability than a casual experimenter

### Attack Path Analysis (APA)
Core analytical tool of DO-356A. Traces the chain of steps an attacker would need to take from an external interface to a safety-critical function. Used to identify:
- Which attack paths are feasible given realistic attacker assumptions
- Where mitigations most effectively break the attack chain
- Whether multiple independent attack paths exist (similar to common cause in safety)
- Which security requirements are most critical

### Connectivity and Attack Surfaces
Modern aircraft attack surfaces requiring assessment:
- **ACARS / SATCOM**: Aircraft-to-airline and ATC data links; also used for weather data, flight plan uplinks
- **Electronic Flight Bag (EFB) interfaces**: Crew-operated tablets/laptops connecting to aircraft systems for charts, performance calculations
- **WAIC (Wireless Avionics Intra-Communication)**: Short-range wireless connections between aircraft systems components (eliminates some wiring)
- **Data loading (ARINC 615A, 615)**: Navigation database updates, software updates via USB or datalink
- **Airport/ground connectivity**: Gate data uplinks for ATIS, ACARS via airport ground network
- **Maintenance laptop connections**: Diagnostic interfaces on aircraft systems
- **Passenger entertainment systems**: If IFE shares any hardware or connectivity with avionics domains

## 4. ASP LIFECYCLE AND DOCUMENTATION

### Certification Phase (DO-326A scope)
During development and certification:
- Security risk assessment performed alongside safety assessment
- Threat conditions identified, classified, and addressed
- Security requirements derived and flowed to system/software development
- Security mitigations implemented and verified
- Certification authority review of security evidence
- Means of compliance agreed in certification plan

### In-Service Phase (DO-355 scope)
After entry into service:
- Vulnerability management: responding to newly discovered cybersecurity vulnerabilities in airborne components
- Security update processes for avionics software
- MRO procedures ensuring security configuration maintained
- Incident reporting and response
- Airworthiness directives for security-related mandated updates

## 5. DO-326A RELATIONSHIP TO DO-178C AND ARP4754A

Security requirements from DO-326A flow to DO-178C (software) and DO-254 (hardware):
- Security-derived software requirements become HLRs/LLRs in DO-178C
- Software implementing security functions (authentication, access control, firewall functionality) is assigned a software level based on its security-criticality combined with its failure condition contribution
- Verification of security requirements follows DO-178C verification process

The ASP runs in parallel with the ARP4754A development assurance process:
- FHA (ARP4754A) identifies functional failure conditions
- Security risk assessment (DO-326A) identifies threat conditions
- Both contribute safety/security requirements to system development
- Both verify that requirements are met in the implementation

## 6. DO-326A INTERVIEW Q&A

**Q1: What is the purpose of DO-326A and how does it relate to ARP4754A?**
A: DO-326A defines the Airworthiness Security Process (ASP) — the framework for identifying, assessing, and mitigating cybersecurity threats that could affect the airworthiness of an aircraft. It relates to ARP4754A in structure but addresses a different threat category: ARP4754A and its associated safety assessment process (ARP4761) address random failures and development errors using probabilistic and development assurance methods. DO-326A addresses intentional adversary-driven threats using threat modeling and attack path analysis. Both are required for modern aircraft with external connectivity, as EASA CS-25 §1319 mandates protection against intentional unauthorized electronic interaction that could adversely affect safety. The threat condition classification in DO-326A uses the same severity categories as ARP4754A failure conditions, linking the security assessment to the existing airworthiness framework.

**Q2: What is the difference between DO-326A and DO-356A?**
A: DO-326A defines the PROCESS — the Airworthiness Security Process lifecycle, what activities must be performed, what documentation is required, and how security evidence is presented to the certification authority. It is analogous to ARP4754A. DO-356A provides the METHODS AND TOOLS — the specific technical methods for implementing the process: how to perform threat modeling, how to conduct Attack Path Analysis, what tools can be used, what specific technical mitigation strategies are appropriate. It is analogous to ARP4761. DO-355 addresses a third dimension: the continuing airworthiness aspect — vulnerability management and security updates after certification. Together the three documents cover the full security lifecycle from development through in-service operations.

**Q3: How does the security risk assessment in DO-326A differ from the safety assessment in ARP4761?**
A: The ARP4761 safety assessment models RANDOM failures and uses probabilistic methods: failure rates (failures per flight hour), fault tree analysis with probability values, and quantitative targets (e.g., <10⁻⁹ per flight hour for catastrophic failure conditions). DO-326A security risk assessment models INTENTIONAL threats and uses adversarial reasoning: attacker capability (technical skill, resources), motivation (nation-state vs script kiddie), opportunity (which interfaces are accessible), and feasibility of attack paths. The severity classification system is identical to ARP4754A (Catastrophic through No Safety Effect), creating a common language for both safety and security contributions to airworthiness. However, the likelihood component is not expressed in quantitative probability terms — instead it's assessed qualitatively based on realistic threat actor assumptions. A key difference: in safety, an unlikely event is one that rarely occurs randomly; in security, an attack might be highly likely if an attacker is motivated and the vulnerability is accessible, regardless of statistical probability.

**Q4: What is an attack path and why is Attack Path Analysis important?**
A: An attack path (analyzed per DO-356A) is the sequence of steps an attacker must take to traverse from an external interface (where they can interact with the aircraft systems) to a safety-critical function (where exploitation could affect airworthiness). Attack Path Analysis (APA) traces these paths systematically to determine: which paths are technically feasible given realistic attacker assumptions; which steps in each path can be mitigated to break the attack chain; whether multiple independent paths exist to the same critical function; and which security requirements are most critical to implement. APA is important because modern avionics systems have complex connectivity, and the indirect paths are often more dangerous than the obvious direct ones. A passenger IFE system that shares network infrastructure with avionics could provide a multi-step attack path that bypasses many direct security controls. APA makes these indirect paths visible so they can be addressed.
