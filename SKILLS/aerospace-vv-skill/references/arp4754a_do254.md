# ARP4754A & DO-254 Deep Reference — System Development & Hardware Design Assurance

---

## PART 1: ARP4754A (ED-79A) — Guidelines for Development of Civil Aircraft and Systems

**Standard:** SAE ARP4754A / EUROCAE ED-79A  
**Date:** December 2010  
**Prepared by:** SAE S-18 / EUROCAE WG-63  
**Supersedes:** ARP4754 / ED-79 (1996)  
**Companion documents:** ARP4761 (safety methods), DO-178C (software), DO-254 (hardware)

---

## 1. SCOPE AND PURPOSE

ARP4754A provides guidelines for the development of civil aircraft and systems from conceptual definition through certification. It:
- Addresses the development cycle for aircraft and systems implementing aircraft functions
- Covers validation of requirements and verification of design implementation for certification
- Does NOT cover: detailed software development (→ DO-178C), electronic hardware development (→ DO-254), system safety assessment process methods (→ ARP4761), in-service safety (→ ARP5150/5151), structural development, MMEL/CDL development
- Applicable to CS-25/14CFR Part 25 context, but applicable to Parts 23, 27, 29, 33, 35

### Why ARP4754A Exists
Development errors in complex/integrated systems cannot be addressed adequately by traditional safety analysis techniques alone. ARP4754A addresses SYSTEMATIC errors (development errors in requirements and design) through a structured development assurance process, complementing the probabilistic failure analysis of ARP4761 which addresses RANDOM hardware failures.

Key philosophical point: There is NO direct correlation between FDAL/IDAL and numerical probability values. Development assurance addresses qualitative confidence in the process, not quantitative failure rates.

---

## 2. KEY DEFINITIONS (§2.2)

**Development Assurance**: All planned and systematic actions used to substantiate, at an adequate level of confidence, that errors in requirements, design, and implementation have been identified and corrected such that the system satisfies the applicable certification basis. (AMC 25)

**FDAL (Function Development Assurance Level)**: The level of rigor of development assurance tasks performed on Functions. Used to identify ARP4754A objectives needing satisfaction for aircraft/system functions.

**IDAL (Item Development Assurance Level)**: The level of rigor of development assurance tasks performed on Items (hardware or software). Equivalent to Software Level in DO-178C for software items; Design Assurance Level in DO-254 for hardware items.

**Failure Condition**: A condition having an effect on the aircraft and/or its occupants, either direct or consequential, caused or contributed to by one or more failures or errors, considering flight phase and relevant adverse operational/environmental conditions or external events.

**Failure**: An occurrence affecting operation of a component such that it can no longer function as intended. Includes both loss of function AND malfunction. NOTE: Errors may CAUSE failures but are not considered failures themselves.

**Error**: An omitted or incorrect action by a crewmember or maintenance person, or a mistake in requirements, design, or implementation.

**Function**: Intended behavior of a product based on a defined set of requirements, regardless of implementation.

**Functional Failure Set (FFS)**: A single member or specific group of members considered independent that lead to a top-level Failure Condition. Equivalent to a Fault Tree minimal cut set — members represent effects of potential development errors rather than hardware failures.

**Independence**: (1) Minimizes likelihood of common mode errors and cascade failures between functions/items; (2) Separation of responsibilities ensuring objective evaluation (validation activities not performed solely by the developer of the requirement).

**Derived Requirements**: Additional requirements resulting from design or implementation decisions during development that are not directly traceable to higher-level requirements.

**Validation**: The determination that requirements for a product are correct and complete. "Are we building the right aircraft/system/function/item?"

**Verification**: The evaluation of an implementation of requirements to determine they have been met. "Did we build the aircraft/system/function/item right?"

## 3. DEVELOPMENT PLANNING (Chapter 3)

### Planning Process (§3.1)
Planning activities:
- Identify regulatory requirements and certification basis
- Define development lifecycle and interfaces
- Establish how development assurance activities integrate into overall program
- Define schedule and resource allocation for integral processes
- Identify suppliers and their oversight

Planning documents include a System Development Plan and Safety Program Plan (§5.1.5, Appendix B). Plans must cover all five integral process areas: safety assessment, development assurance level assignment, requirements capture, requirements validation, implementation verification, configuration management, process assurance, and certification coordination.

### Transition Criteria (§3.2)
Transition criteria control movement between development phases. Deviations from plans must be formally identified, approved, and documented.

## 4. AIRCRAFT AND SYSTEM DEVELOPMENT PROCESS (Chapter 4)

### Development Life Cycle Model (§4.1)
Top-down, iterative process:
1. **Concept**: Aircraft performance/configuration, payload/range, technologies
2. **Aircraft Function Development (§4.2)**: Identification of aircraft-level functions (e.g., Flight Control, Navigation, Engine Control), their requirements and interfaces
3. **Allocation of Aircraft Functions to Systems (§4.3)**: Grouping functions; allocating requirements to systems; defining interfaces (all inputs have a source; all outputs have destination(s))
4. **Development of System Architecture (§4.4)**: Evaluated using PASA/PSSA and CCA; candidate architectures evaluated iteratively
5. **Allocation of System Requirements to Items (§4.5)**: Iterative; complete when all requirements accommodated within final architecture; outputs IDAL assignments for hardware and software items
6. **System Implementation (§4.6)**: HW/SW design/build, HW/SW integration, aircraft/system integration

### Information Flow System→Item (§4.6.1.1)
Data passed to HW and SW processes:
- Requirements allocated to hardware
- Requirements allocated to software
- Development assurance level(s) for items with description of associated Failure Condition(s)
- Allocated failure rates and exposure intervals for hardware functional failures
- System description
- Design constraints (function isolation, separation, partitioning requirements, item development independence requirements)
- System verification activities to be performed at item level

### Information Flow Item→System (§4.6.1.2)
Data passed from HW/SW processes to system:
- Derived requirements (both HW and SW) to be evaluated against system/item requirements and safety assessments
- Description of implemented HW/SW architecture (segregation, partitioning)
- Evidence of verification activities performed at item level
- Hardware failure rates, fault detection coverage, CCA data, latency intervals
- Problem/change documentation
- Limitations of use, configuration identification/status constraints

## 5. INTEGRAL PROCESSES (Chapter 5)

### 5.1 Safety Assessment

The safety assessment process demonstrates compliance with airworthiness regulations (14CFR/CS §1309) and internal safety standards. Primary processes per ARP4761:

#### Functional Hazard Assessment (FHA) — §5.1.1
Conducted at BOTH aircraft AND system levels. The FHA:
- Identifies Failure Conditions for each function
- Classifies each Failure Condition: Catastrophic / Hazardous/Severe-Major / Major / Minor / No Safety Effect
- States what was considered and what assumptions were made (adverse conditions, flight phase)
- Is a LIVING DOCUMENT updated throughout development as new functions or Failure Conditions identified
- At aircraft level: identifies aircraft-level FCs
- At system level: decomposes aircraft-level FCs to system functions

When aircraft-level functions are integrated by a system or combination of systems, FHA must be re-evaluated to identify FCs involving multiple functions. Common causes across system or function boundaries must be reviewed.

**Quantitative probability requirements per AC 25.1309-1A (for transport category):**
| Failure Condition | Probability Requirement |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| Catastrophic | < 10⁻⁹ per flight hour |
| Hazardous | < 10⁻⁷ per flight hour |
| Major | < 10⁻⁵ per flight hour |
| Minor | < 10⁻³ per flight hour |
| No Safety Effect | No requirement |

#### Preliminary Aircraft/System Safety Assessment (PASA/PSSA) — §5.1.2
Systematic examination of PROPOSED ARCHITECTURE(S) to determine how failures could cause the FCs identified by the FHA. Objectives:
- Complete safety requirements of aircraft/system/item
- Validate that proposed architecture can reasonably meet safety requirements
- Identify need for alternative protective strategies (partitioning, BIT, monitoring, independence, maintenance intervals)
- Determine safety requirements for hardware and software items (IDALs)

The PSSA is iterative — conducted at multiple stages as design definition progresses. At lowest level, PSSA determines safety-related design requirements of HW and SW.

PSSA outputs used as inputs to: ASA/SSA, system requirements, software requirements, hardware requirements.

**Key distinction: PSSA evaluates the PROPOSED architecture; SSA verifies the IMPLEMENTED design.**

#### Aircraft/System Safety Assessment (ASA/SSA) — §5.1.3
Systematic, comprehensive evaluation of the IMPLEMENTED aircraft and system(s) showing relevant safety requirements are satisfied. The SSA verifies:
- All safety activities performed per Safety Plan
- Safety requirements satisfied with supporting material available
- Safety validation/verification process complete and results accepted
- Activities form a logical argument that the aircraft/system is acceptably safe

SSA process data includes:
- List of previously agreed external event probabilities
- System description including functions and interfaces
- List of Failure Conditions from FHA/PASA/PSSA
- Qualitative analyses (FTA, FMES, Markov Analysis, Dependence Diagrams)
- Quantitative analyses (FTA, FMES, Markov Analysis, Dependence Diagrams)
- Results from CCA
- Safety-related tasks and maintenance intervals
- FDAL for aircraft functions and systems (from PASA/PSSA)
- IDAL for electronic hardware and software items (from PSSA)
- Verification that PASA/PSSA safety requirements incorporated into design and testing
- Results of non-analytic verification (test, demonstration, inspection)

The Aircraft Safety Assessment forms a "safety case" — a clear, comprehensible, defensible argument that the aircraft/systems are acceptably safe.

#### Common Cause Analysis (CCA) — §5.1.4
Establishes and verifies physical and functional separation, isolation, and independence between systems and items. **Common cause events shall be precluded for catastrophic failure conditions.**

Three CCA sub-analyses:

**Particular Risks Analysis:**
Events or influences external to the system(s)/item(s) that may violate failure independence claims. Required by airworthiness regulations. Examples: bird strike, lightning, engine burst, high-energy rotor burst, fire, cargo fire. See ARP4761 Appendix J.

**Common Mode Analysis (CMA):**
Verifies that failure events identified in FTA/SSA are INDEPENDENT in the actual implementation. Effects of development, manufacturing, installation, maintenance and crew errors, and failures of system components that defeat independence must be analyzed. Identical systems/items susceptible to common failures. Results of preliminary CMA are KEY to DAL assignment. See ARP4761 Appendix K.

**Zonal Safety Analysis (ZSA):**
Analysis of each zone of the aircraft regarding basic installation, interference between systems, and maintenance errors. See ARP4761 Appendix I.

**Note:** ZSA and Particular Risks Analysis are NOT used to assign Development Assurance Levels — only CMA results feed into FDAL/IDAL assignment.

#### Safety Program Plan (§5.1.5)
Must cover:
- Input requirements at aircraft level
- Applicable safety standards
- Safety organization and responsibilities
- Safety activities and deliverables
- Key project milestones for reports
- Principles for managing, validating safety requirements, and verifying design meets requirements
- Links to other plans (certification plan, V&V plan, process assurance plan)

### 5.2 Development Assurance Level Assignment

#### Fundamental Philosophy (§5.2)
**Development Assurance Level (DAL)** is assigned to mitigate ERRORS (development mistakes in requirements, design, implementation) — NOT random failures. The safety assessment process (§5.1) addresses random failure probability requirements. These are two separate, complementary approaches.

The more severe the Failure Condition Classification → the greater the Development Assurance necessary.

#### FDAL vs IDAL (§5.2.2)

**Function development phase:**
- Requirements for Functions are developed and allocated to items
- Level of rigor for Function requirement development = **FDAL**
- Objectives per ARP4754A; applicability per Appendix A

**Item development phase:**
- Items (hardware or software) are developed
- Level of rigor for item development = **IDAL**
- Objectives for software items: per DO-178C software levels
- Objectives for hardware items: per DO-254 DALs
- An item does NOT contain architectural features used for credit in mitigating potential development errors WITHIN ITSELF — that credit is at the system/function level

**Boundaries between systems and items may not coincide with organizational boundaries (manufacturer ↔ supplier).**

#### General Principles for DAL Assignment (§5.2.1)

**Catastrophic Failure Condition:**
- Single function alone can cause it → assign DAL A to that function's development process
- COMBINATION of two or more independently developed functions needed → EITHER one DAL A OR two at least DAL B. Other independently developed functions: no lower than DAL C. The development process ESTABLISHING that the two functions are independent: must remain DAL A.

**Hazardous/Severe-Major Failure Condition:**
- Single function → at least DAL B
- Combination of two or more → EITHER one at least DAL B OR two at least DAL C. Other functions: no lower than DAL D. Independence-establishing process: must remain DAL B.

**Major Failure Condition:**
- Single function → DAL C
- Combination of two or more → EITHER one at least DAL C OR two at least DAL D. Independence-establishing process: must remain DAL C.

**Minor Failure Condition:**
- Single function → at least DAL D
- Combination of two or more → at least one DAL D

#### Table 2 — Top-Level Function FDAL Assignment
| Top-Level FC Severity | Top-Level Function FDAL |
|-----------------------|------------------------|
| Catastrophic | A |
| Hazardous/Severe-Major | B |
| Major | C |
| Minor | D |
| No Safety Effect | E |

#### FDAL Assignment with System Architecture Consideration (§5.2.3.2)
Uses Functional Failure Sets (FFS) — conceptually equivalent to Fault Tree minimal cut sets, where members represent potential development errors rather than hardware failures.

**FDAL Assignment Process:**
1. Begin with FDAL assigned to top-level function based on FC severity (Table 2)
2. Use FTA/Dependence Diagrams to identify all FFS that lead to each top-level FC
3. For each FFS, assess whether members are functionally independent (different requirements reducing likelihood of common requirement error) and developmentally independent (different development processes/teams)
4. Apply independence credit to assign lower FDALs to sub-functions where justified

**Independence Types (§5.2.3.2.1):**

*Functional Independence (§5.2.3.2.1.1)*: Functions are different to minimize likelihood of common REQUIREMENT error. Different functional requirements minimize likelihood of same error in both.

*Item Development Independence (§5.2.3.2.1.2)*: Items developed by different teams with different processes to minimize likelihood of common DESIGN/IMPLEMENTATION error.

**When FFS has one member**: That member's FDAL = FC severity classification's associated level. No independence credit.

**When FFS has two or more members AND functional independence demonstrated**: FDAL reduction may be taken. Members may be assigned one level lower than the top-level function's FDAL.

**When FFS has two or more members AND functional independence NOT demonstrated**: No FDAL reduction — all members must be assigned the top-level function's FDAL.

**Item Level (IDAL) Assignment:**
IDALs are assigned to hardware items and software items based on the FDALs of the functions they implement, considering item-level architecture. IDALs are then used to drive DO-178C (software) and DO-254 (hardware) process rigor.

Interactions of system Functions making up an Aircraft Function assessed at the FDAL of the aircraft Function. Interactions of items making up a system Function assessed at the HIGHER of the FDALs of the aircraft and system Functions.

### 5.3 Requirements Capture (§5.3)

#### Types of Requirements (§5.3.1)
- **Safety requirements**: Derived from safety analyses; include quantitative probability requirements, qualitative independence requirements, monitoring requirements, FDAL/IDAL requirements, operational/maintenance requirements
- **Derived requirements**: Requirements arising from design/implementation decisions not directly traceable to higher-level requirements — must be validated and safety impact determined
- **Functional requirements**: What the system must do
- **Performance requirements**: How well the system must do it
- **Interface requirements**: What the system must interact with
- **Operational requirements**: How the system is operated
- **Maintenance requirements**: What is required to maintain the system

All requirements must be traceable to their source and validated.

#### Deriving Safety Requirements from Safety Analyses (§5.3.2)
Safety analyses generate safety requirements at multiple levels:
- Aircraft level: from aircraft FHA
- System level: from system FHA (decomposition of aircraft-level)
- Item level: from PSSA; allocated to HW and SW via IDALs

**Critical rule**: Derived requirements from SW/HW processes (§4.6.1.2.a) must be fed back to the system process for evaluation against system/item requirements and safety assessments. If a SW design decision introduces new behavior not in the PSSA → the PSSA must be updated.

---

### 5.4 Requirements Validation (§5.4)

**Validation**: Determining requirements are CORRECT and COMPLETE. "Are we building the right system?"
**Verification**: Determining implementation MEETS requirements. "Did we build it right?"

#### Validation Process Objectives (§5.4.1)
- All requirements correctly and completely capture the desired system function and performance
- All safety requirements are correctly defined and traceable to safety analyses
- Requirements are unambiguous, consistent, verifiable, and achievable

#### Validation Methods (§5.4.6)
- Reviews and inspections (including independent reviews)
- Simulation and modeling
- Prototype evaluation
- Similarity analysis (comparison to previously certified similar systems)
- Engineering analysis
- Flight test (for certain functional requirements)
- Test (where requirements can be tested directly)

#### Correctness Checks (§5.4.3)
Requirements must be correct with respect to:
- External interfaces (aircraft, environment, crew)
- System architecture and implementation constraints
- Operational usage scenarios
- Previous-level requirements (requirements at lower level satisfy higher-level requirements)

#### Completeness Checks (§5.4.4)
Requirements complete when:
- All functions identified in the FHA/PSSA are specified
- All safety requirements are captured
- All operating modes and conditions (normal, abnormal, failure modes) are covered
- All external interfaces are defined
- All derived requirements are identified

#### Validation Rigor (§5.4.5)
Rigor of validation activity proportional to the FDAL of the function and the severity of potential errors. Higher FDAL → more formal validation methods (multiple independent reviews, simulation, more comprehensive test coverage).

---

### 5.5 Implementation Verification (§5.5)

**Verification**: Confirming the implementation meets the requirements — that we built it right.

#### Verification Process Objectives (§5.5.1)
- Verification provides evidence that requirements are correctly and completely implemented
- All safety requirements are verified to be met
- Verification coverage is appropriate for the assigned development assurance levels
- Evidence of verification is traceable and retrievable

#### Verification Methods (§5.5.5)
- **Test**: Quantitative procedure with objective pass/fail criteria — applies stimuli and observes outputs
- **Analysis**: Detailed examination including: FTA, FMEA, reliability analysis, timing analysis, simulation
- **Review/Inspection**: Qualitative evaluation of requirements, design data, implementation — includes code reviews, design reviews
- **Demonstration**: Observing operation — less formal than test
- **Similarity**: Comparison to previously verified similar systems (must justify applicability)

#### Verification Rigor (§5.5.3)
Proportional to the IDAL of the item. Higher IDAL → more independence required, more formal verification methods, more complete coverage, more extensive documentation.

For items with IDALs: verification objectives are those of DO-178C (software) or DO-254 (hardware) for the respective level.

#### Verification Data (§5.5.6)
Documentation of verification results including:
- Verification plans, procedures, and results
- Traceability between requirements, implementation, and verification evidence
- Problem reports from verification activities with resolution
- Tool qualification data for verification tools

### 5.6 Configuration Management (§5.6)

Similar philosophy to DO-178C §7. Objectives:
- Each item and successive versions unambiguously labeled
- Baselines defined for further development and as reference points
- Change control ensures problems recorded, evaluated, resolved, approved
- Status accounting provides visibility of configuration status

CM activities: configuration identification, baseline establishment, problem reporting/tracking/corrective action, change control, release/archive/retrieval.

### 5.7 Process Assurance (§5.7)

Ensures life cycle process objectives are met and activities completed per plans. Requires independence. Activities include:
- Project plan reviews (§5.7.3): confirm plans are adequate, complete, and in agreement
- Evidence of process assurance (§5.7.4): audits, reports showing compliance with plans

### 5.8 Certification and Regulatory Authority Coordination (§5.8)

#### Certification Planning (§5.8.1)
Establish certification basis with authority; develop Plan for Civil Aviation Authority Coordination (or equivalent); identify IPs (Issue Papers) or special conditions early.

#### Agreement on Means of Compliance (§5.8.2)
Propose means of compliance in certification plan; obtain agreement from authority on approach before committing to full implementation.

#### Compliance Substantiation (§5.8.3)
Provide evidence that all development activities satisfy agreed plans. Reviews may occur at applicant's or supplier's facilities.

#### Certification Data (§5.8.4)
Minimum certification data includes:
- Aircraft or System Development Plan (including all integral process plans)
- Safety Assessment data (FHA, PSSA, SSA, CCA) per ARP4761
- Data substantiating compliance with all development assurance level objectives (per Appendix A)
- Results of validation and verification activities
- Problem reports and resolution records
- Configuration management records
- Certificate of Design Conformance

Safety-related flight operations or maintenance tasks must be documented in type design data (ICA — Instructions for Continued Airworthiness).

## 6. MODIFICATIONS TO AIRCRAFT OR SYSTEMS (Chapter 6)

### Modification Process Overview (§6.1)
Any change to certified aircraft/system requires assessment of impact on: original certification basis, safety assessment, FDAL/IDAL assignments, validation/verification evidence.

### Modification Impact Analysis (§6.3)
Impact analysis determines: what changed, what other functions/systems could be affected, whether safety assessment needs updating, what reverification is required.

### Modification Categorization (§6.4)
Categories from "minor modification" to "major modification" (in US terms STC/MajorAlteration) affect extent of certification authority involvement and evidence required.

### Key Modification Scenarios (§6.6)
- **New aircraft-level function (§6.6.1)**: Full process from FHA through implementation for the new function; assess interaction with all existing functions; revise aircraft-level FHA
- **Replacing item/system on existing aircraft (§6.6.2)**: Assess compatibility; if same FDAL/IDAL and no functional change → limited analysis; if functional change → repeat relevant development phases
- **Adapting item/system to different aircraft type (§6.6.3)**: New aircraft-level FHA may result in different FDAL; reverification may be required; compatibility with new aircraft HW/SW interfaces
- **Modification without adding function (§6.6.4)**: Impact analysis; typically reverification of affected area

## 7. ARP4754A INTERVIEW Q&A

**Q1: What is the fundamental difference between ARP4754A and ARP4761?**
A: ARP4754A provides guidelines for the DEVELOPMENT ASSURANCE process — ensuring that requirements, design, and implementation are developed with sufficient rigor to prevent SYSTEMATIC ERRORS. ARP4761 provides guidelines for the SAFETY ASSESSMENT PROCESS — methods for identifying Failure Conditions and demonstrating that RANDOM FAILURE probabilities meet quantitative targets. They are complementary: ARP4761 addresses hardware failure probabilities through FHA/PSSA/SSA/CCA; ARP4754A addresses development process quality through FDALs, validation, verification, and process assurance. Both are needed for a complete certification argument.

**Q2: Explain the difference between FDAL and IDAL with an example.**
A: FDAL (Functional Development Assurance Level) is assigned to an AIRCRAFT/SYSTEM FUNCTION based on the severity of the Failure Condition that could result from an error in developing that function's requirements (per Table 2). IDAL (Item Development Assurance Level) is assigned to the HARDWARE OR SOFTWARE ITEMS that implement the function, based on the FDAL and the system architecture.

Example: "Loss of pitch control" is an aircraft-level function with FDAL A (Catastrophic FC). The flight control computer (FCU) software item implementing pitch control might receive IDAL A (→ DO-178C Level A). But if the architecture has two independent FCUs, each whose failure alone only causes a Hazardous FC, the software in each FCU might receive IDAL B (→ DO-178C Level B), provided independence is demonstrated via CMA.

**Q3: Can IDAL ever be lower than FDAL? What conditions allow this?**
A: Yes — IDAL can be assigned LOWER than FDAL if the system architecture provides independence between items such that no single item's error alone causes the top-level Failure Condition. Per §5.2.1, if a Catastrophic FC requires a COMBINATION of development errors in two or more independently developed functions: both functions can be assigned IDAL B instead of IDAL A. The process ESTABLISHING and VERIFYING that independence: must remain FDAL A. Independence must be demonstrated through Common Mode Analysis (§5.1.4).

If independence between software components cannot be demonstrated → all components assigned the most severe IDAL (equivalent to DO-178C §2.3 partitioning rules).

**Q4: What is a Functional Failure Set and how is it used in FDAL assignment?**
A: A Functional Failure Set (FFS, §5.2.3.2) is a set of aircraft/system function members — each considered independent — that together lead to a top-level Failure Condition. It is conceptually equivalent to a Fault Tree Analysis minimal cut set, but represents DEVELOPMENT ERRORS rather than hardware failures.

The FFS is used by: identifying all FFSs that lead to each top-level FC using FTA/Dependence Diagrams; assessing whether FFS members are functionally and developmentally independent; applying independence credit to reduce FDALs of members where justified.

Example: FC "Loss of all thrust" has an FFS containing {Engine Control System, Fuel Control Unit Software}. If these are developed independently (functional independence: different requirement sets; development independence: different teams/tools), each might be assigned a lower FDAL than the top-level FC's severity would otherwise require.

**Q5: What is the difference between validation and verification in ARP4754A?**
A: Validation (§5.4) answers "Are we building the RIGHT system?" — ensuring requirements are CORRECT and COMPLETE with respect to the intended function. It occurs BEFORE or DURING implementation, and examines requirements against operational needs, safety analysis results, and the aircraft-level functions. Example: reviewing whether the FCC requirements correctly capture the required pitch authority, rate limits, and failure responses.

Verification (§5.5) answers "Did we build it RIGHT?" — ensuring the IMPLEMENTATION meets the requirements. It occurs DURING and AFTER implementation. Example: testing that the FCC software actually limits pitch rate as specified in the LLRs; reviewing source code against software design standards.

Both are required; both are proportional to the FDAL/IDAL assigned to the item.

**Q6: Describe the safety assessment process sequence in ARP4754A.**
A: The safety assessment process is iterative throughout development:

**FHA** (§5.1.1) — earliest: identify and classify Failure Conditions for aircraft and system functions; assign preliminary FDAL. Living document updated as design evolves.

**PASA/PSSA** (§5.1.2) — during architecture development: systematically examine proposed architectures to determine how failures cause FCs from FHA; validate architectures can meet safety requirements; derive safety requirements for systems and items (IDALs); identify independence and redundancy requirements. Uses FTA, Dependence Diagrams.

**CCA** (§5.1.4) — concurrent with PSSA and throughout: verify independence claims (Common Mode Analysis); identify particular risk exposure (Particular Risks Analysis); confirm zonal installation meets safety requirements (Zonal Safety Analysis).

**ASA/SSA** (§5.1.3) — after implementation: verify implemented system meets requirements from FHA and PSSA; integrate all analysis results (FTA, FMEA, CCA); confirm quantitative probability requirements met; form the safety case.

**Q7: What must be fed back from software processes to the system safety assessment?**
A: Per §4.6.1.2, several items must be fed back from software (and hardware) processes to the system process:
- **Derived requirements**: SW design decisions creating new requirements not in the original system requirements. These must be evaluated against system/item requirements and the safety assessment — a SW derived requirement might introduce a new failure mode not in the PSSA.
- **Description of implemented SW architecture**: showing achieved independence and fault containment (e.g., partitioning details)
- **Evidence of system/item verification activities performed at SW level**
- **Hardware failure rates, fault detection coverage, CCA data, latency intervals** (for hardware items)
- **Problem/change documentation**: changes impacting system or item requirements or safety assessments
- **Limitations of use and configuration constraints**

This feedback loop is critical: if the SSA was built on assumptions about the SW architecture (e.g., assuming a watchdog timer with a specific response time), and the SW design implements a different response time, the SSA must be updated.

**Q8: What is the Safety Program Plan and what does it cover?**
A: The Safety Program Plan (§5.1.5, Appendix B) defines the scope and content of safety activities at the aircraft level. It covers:
- Input requirements at aircraft level for which safety design/analysis responsibility is taken
- Applicable safety standards (CS-25, ARP4761, etc.)
- Safety organization and responsibilities (including relationship with partners/suppliers)
- Safety activities and deliverables
- Key project milestones for reports
- Principles for managing, validating safety requirements, and verifying design meets them
- Links to other plans (certification plan, V&V plan, process assurance plan)

The safety program plan may also be applied at system level.

## PART 2: DO-254 — Design Assurance Guidance for Airborne Electronic Hardware

**Standard:** RTCA DO-254 / EUROCAE ED-80  
**Date:** April 19, 2000  
**Prepared by:** SC-180 (RTCA) / WG-46 (EUROCAE)  
**Scope:** Complex electronic hardware — FPGAs, ASICs, PLDs, LRUs, circuit board assemblies

---

## 1. PURPOSE AND SCOPE

DO-254 provides design assurance guidance for airborne electronic hardware such that it safely performs its intended function in its specified environments. It covers:
- Hardware design assurance OBJECTIVES (not how the design should be implemented)
- Activities that should be performed to reduce probability of design and implementation errors
- Hardware design life cycle processes from conception through post-certification improvements
- LRUs, circuit board assemblies, ASICs, PLDs, multi-chip modules, COTS components

**NOT covered:** Firmware (must be classified as HW or SW and addressed by applicable processes), environmental qualification (→ DO-160G), organizational structures, personnel qualification.

### Simple vs Complex Hardware (§1.6)
**Simple hardware**: A comprehensive combination of deterministic tests and analyses appropriate to the DAL can ensure correct functional performance under ALL foreseeable conditions with NO anomalous behavior. No specific quantitative definition — determined by engineering judgment.

**Complex hardware**: When simple classification cannot be demonstrated. An item constructed entirely of simple items may itself be complex. Items with ASICs or PLDs can be simple if they meet the simple criteria. **For complex items, design assurance strategy must be agreed with certification authority EARLY in the life cycle.**

Main impact of DO-254: complex hardware items, especially ASICs and PLDs.

---

## 2. SYSTEM ASPECTS OF HARDWARE DESIGN ASSURANCE (§2)

### Hardware Design Assurance Levels (Table 2-1)
| HDAL | FC Classification | Description |
|------|------------------|-------------|
| **A** | Catastrophic | HW functions whose failure would cause catastrophic FC |
| **B** | Hazardous/Severe-Major | HW functions whose failure would cause hazardous FC |
| **C** | Major | HW functions whose failure would cause major FC |
| **D** | Minor | HW functions whose failure would cause minor FC |
| **E** | No Effect | No safety effect; no DO-254 guidance required |

### Hardware Safety Assessment (§2.3)

#### Hardware Safety Assessment Considerations (§2.3.1)
A hardware item may have separate Functional Failure Paths (FFPs) to accommodate different design assurance levels or strategies. If a hardware item contains functions with DIFFERENT DALs:
- **Option 1**: Assure the entire item at the HIGHEST DAL
- **Option 2**: Assure individual functions separately at their respective DALs, IF their functions, interfaces, and shared resources can be protected from adverse effects of lower-DAL functions. Shared resources: assurance at the HIGHEST DAL.

Derived hardware safety requirements include (§2.3.1):
- Circuit/component redundancy
- Separation or electrical isolation between circuits
- Dissimilarity between circuits/components
- Monitoring of circuits/components
- Protection or reconfiguration mechanisms
- Allowed failure rates and probabilities for random failures and latent failures
- Limitations of usage or installation
- Prevention and management of upsets and upset recovery

#### Quantitative Assessment of Random Faults (§2.3.2)
Statistical failure assessment (failure rates, redundancy, failure mode statistics, probability analysis, component de-rating) is acceptable for random hardware failures. These are statistically predictable.

#### Qualitative Assessment of Design Errors and Upsets (§2.3.3)
Unlike random failures, design errors and upsets are NOT statistically predictable. Qualitative safety assessment methods (FTA, common mode analysis, functional FMEA) determine effects of design errors and upsets and means to preclude or mitigate them. Redundancy management must address potential common mode faults.

#### Design Assurance Strategy Selection Process — Figure 2-3 (§2.3.4)
Sequential decision process:
1. Determine FFP design assurance level for each hardware item function
2. If Level A or B → determine if HW is simple or complex
3. If complex AND Level A or B → develop design assurance strategy per Appendix B
4. Assess if strategy is adequate; modify if not
5. Document fail-safe aspects
6. Document and obtain certification authority approval of design assurance approach in PHAC
7. Implement approach

For Level A or B complex FFPs: advanced analysis, product service experience, OR architectural mitigation must be used.

## 5. HARDWARE DESIGN PROCESSES (§5)

Figure 5-1 shows five sequential processes:

### Requirements Capture (§5.1)
Identifies and records hardware item requirements including derived requirements from architecture, technology choices, functionality, environmental, and performance requirements, plus requirements from system safety assessment.

**Requirements Capture Objectives (§5.1.1):**
- Requirements identified, defined, documented (including PSSA requirements and derived requirements)
- Derived requirements fed back to appropriate process (SSA)
- Requirement omissions and errors provided to appropriate process

**Requirements Capture Activities (§5.1.2):**
- Document system requirements allocated to hardware (functionality, performance, architectural considerations: BIT, testability, external interfaces, environment, test/maintenance, power, physical characteristics)
- Identify safety requirements from PSSA (DALs, probabilistic requirements, architectural/functional safety attributes)
- Identify design constraints (production, standards, technology, design environment)
- Determine derived requirements necessary for implementation — safety-significant derived requirements UNIQUELY identified
- Feed derived requirements back to SSA process
- Document requirements in quantitative terms with tolerances
- Establish traceability to next higher hierarchical level; derived requirements traced as far as possible through hierarchy

### Conceptual Design (§5.2)
Produces high-level design concept for assessment. May include functional block diagrams, design/architecture descriptions, circuit card assembly outlines, chassis sketches.

**Objectives:**
- HW item conceptual design consistent with requirements
- Derived requirements produced fed back to requirements capture
- Requirement omissions/errors provided to appropriate processes for resolution

**Activities:**
- Generate high-level description (architectural constraints for safety, implementation constraints on SW)
- Identify major components and how they contribute to safety requirements (including unused functions)
- Feed derived requirements including interface definition back to requirements capture

### Detailed Design (§5.3)
Produces detailed design data using requirements and conceptual design as basis.

**Objectives:**
- Detailed design developed from requirements and conceptual design
- Derived requirements fed back; omissions/errors provided for resolution

**Activities:**
- Generate detailed design data (assembly/interconnection data, component data, HDL, test methods, HW/SW interface data)
- Implement architectural design techniques (safety monitors, dissimilarity, preclusion of design error impact, fault tolerant designs)
- Design test features where necessary for verification of safety requirements
- Assess unused functions for potential safety effects
- Identify constraints on design, installation, or operation that could affect safety

### Implementation (§5.4)
Uses detailed design to produce hardware item as input to testing.

**Objectives:**
- HW item produced implementing detailed design using representative manufacturing processes
- Implementation, assembly, installation data complete
- Derived requirements fed back; omissions/errors provided for resolution

**Activities:**
- Produce HW item using design data and production-representative resources (procurement, kitting, build, inspection, test)

### Production Transition (§5.5)
Moves product into production. Manufacturing data, test facilities, general resources examined.

**Objectives:**
- Baseline established including all design and manufacturing data needed for consistent replication
- Manufacturing requirements related to safety identified, documented, and controlled
- Derived requirements and errors fed back

**Activities:**
- Prepare manufacturing data from configured design data
- Check for completeness and consistency with configured design data
- Evaluate changes during production transition for adherence to requirements (especially safety)
- Explicitly define manufacturing requirements pertaining to safety for production control

## 6. VALIDATION AND VERIFICATION PROCESS (§6)

### Validation Process (§6.1)
Ensures derived requirements are correct and complete with respect to system requirements allocated to the hardware. NOT intended to validate system-level requirements (that's the system process). Not all derived requirements need validation — focus on: design decisions affecting system safety/functional requirements, and design decisions constraining subsequent design tasks.

**Validation Process Objectives (§6.1.1):**
- Derived requirements against which HW item verified are correct and complete
- Derived requirements evaluated for safety impact
- Omissions and errors fed back for resolution

**Validation Process Activities (§6.1.2):**
Methods: reviews, simulation, prototyping, modeling, analysis, service experience, engineering assessment, tests.
- Identify derived requirements needing validation
- For each: validate by review, analysis, or test at some hierarchical level; validation is appropriate for the requirement; results correct with discrepancies explained
- Evaluate derived requirements for safety impact
- Evaluate completeness and correctness of derived requirements with respect to allocated system requirements
- Establish traceability between derived requirements and validation activities/results

### Verification Process (§6.2)
Provides assurance that hardware item implementation meets ALL requirements. Applies reviews, analyses, and tests per verification plan.

**Verification Process Objectives (§6.2.1):**
- Evidence that HW implementation meets requirements
- Traceability established between requirements, implementation, verification procedures and results
- Acceptance test criteria identified, implementable, consistent with DALs
- Omissions and errors fed back

**Verification Process Activities (§6.2.2):**
- Identify requirements needing verification (not required at every hierarchical level — can verify at higher level)
- Select and perform verification methods (tests, simulation, prototyping, analyses, reviews)
- Establish traceability (requirement → implementation → verification procedure → result); detail proportional to DAL
- Perform coverage analysis to confirm completeness
- Document results; feed back omissions/errors

### Verification Methods (§6.3)

#### Test (§6.3.1)
Confirms hardware correctly responds to stimuli. Examples: functional test on hardware item, system bench test, system validation facility test, aircraft test. May use manual, automated, or specialized equipment; may use built-in test capabilities.

Requirements for test credit:
- Identify each requirement to be tested
- Define testing stimulus, sequence, conditions
- Define pass/fail criteria BEFORE test execution
- Record complete identification of test equipment and calibration dates
- Record configuration identity of hardware item being tested
- Record and retain test results
- Feed test failures back for resolution

#### Analysis (§6.3.2)
Detailed, repeatable, analytical evaluation of specific hardware characteristics. Examples:
- **Thermal Analysis**: Verifies design meets requirements under operating thermal environment
- **Stress Analysis**: Verifies components meet de-rating criteria over required operating range
- **Reliability Analysis**: Establishes whether design satisfies reliability requirements
- **Design Margin Analysis**: Verifies design satisfies functional requirements given component variability
- **Similarity Analysis**: Compares to previously certified similar systems
- **Simulation Analysis**: Compares simulation results to expected results

Simulation is important but CANNOT be used for certification credit without supporting evidence of model validity.

#### Reviews (§6.3.3)
Qualitative evaluation of plans, requirements, design data, design concept, or design implementation. All reviews for certification credit identified in verification plan.

**Requirements Review (§6.3.3.1)**: Ensure acceptability of requirements — each requirement unambiguous, verifiable, complete for its hierarchical level, no conflicts; derived requirements consistent with source; requirements compatible with SSA; software/hardware interface requirements defined; requirements traceable upward; implementation, test, and maintenance feasibility addressed.

**Design Review (§6.3.3.2)**: Determine design data and implementation satisfy requirements. Multiple reviews during lifecycle (conceptual, detail, implementation). Must address: all requirements; environmental requirements; safety/reliability requirements; explicit safety design aspects; ability to implement, test, and maintain; new manufacturing techniques; component selection criteria; traceability to requirements.

## 7. CONFIGURATION MANAGEMENT PROCESS (§7)

### Hardware Configuration Management Activities (§7.2)
- **Configuration Identification (§7.2.1)**: Unique labeling of each CI; identification established before CI used in baseline, referenced by other data, or used for manufacturing. Note: COTS components and previously developed HW must be identified before used in baseline.
- **Baseline Establishment (§7.2.2)**: Baselines for CIs used for certification credit; under change control once established; traceability to previous baseline when certification credit sought
- **Problem Reporting, Tracking, Corrective Action (§7.2.3)**: Implement no later than establishment of baseline from which certification credit sought. Not all PRs must be closed for certification — but all must be evaluated; safety/certification impacting PRs must be closed.
- **Change Control (§7.2.4)**: Recording, evaluation, resolution, approval of changes; start no later than baseline used for certification credit. Change control ensures traceability and impact assessment.
- **Release, Archive, Retrieve (§7.2.5)**: CIs identified and released before manufacture use; data retrievable from approved source; duplicate copies in separate archives; storage media integrity maintained.

### Hardware Control Categories (§7.3)
**HC1**: Full CM activities (configuration identification, baselines, baseline traceability, problem reporting, full change control including records/approvals, release, retrieval, data retention, unauthorized change protection, media selection).

**HC2**: Subset (configuration identification, retrieval, data retention, unauthorized change protection). HC2 data not expected to change incrementally — superseded by new data rather than revised.

Appendix A Table A-1 specifies control category per data item per DAL. Example: Hardware requirements → HC1 at all DALs. Review and analysis results → HC2 at all DALs.

Tools used for design and verification must be assessed. Qualification needed when tool output used without verification and could affect safety. ## 12. APPENDIX B — DESIGN ASSURANCE FOR LEVEL A AND B FUNCTIONS

For Level A or B complex hardware, the following additional strategies apply:

### Functional Failure Path Analysis (FFPA, Appendix B §2)
Divides the hardware item into separate FFPs based on function, design assurance level, technology, or service history. Allows justifying a lower DAL for portions of the hardware item or different strategies for different functional paths.

### Architectural Mitigation (Appendix B §3.1)
Architectural features providing protection against design errors:
- Redundancy with demonstrated independence
- Dissimilarity between redundant channels
- Monitoring with independence from monitored function
- Safety monitoring
- Fault tolerant design

Independence must be demonstrated via CCA and CMA.

### Advanced Analysis Methods (Appendix B §3.3)
When architectural mitigation alone is insufficient, advanced analysis may be required:
- Formal verification methods (mathematical proof of correctness)
- Exhaustive simulation (for bounded input/output spaces)
- Additional structural coverage analysis on HDL or gate-level designs

## 14. DO-254 INTERVIEW Q&A

**Q1: When does DO-254 apply vs when is simple hardware or COTS acceptable?**
A: DO-254 applies to "complex electronic hardware" — primarily FPGAs, ASICs, PLDs, and circuit board assemblies where a comprehensive combination of deterministic tests and analyses cannot ensure correct functional performance under all foreseeable conditions. Simple hardware (where comprehensive deterministic tests/analyses CAN ensure correct performance with no anomalous behavior) has reduced overhead — primarily verification and CM are needed, not extensive design documentation. COTS components (§11.2) may be acceptable with a component management plan covering qualification, acceptance testing, failure reporting, and obsolescence management, even if the COTS supplier doesn't follow DO-254 — but the applicant is responsible for managing the COTS components appropriately.

**Q2: What is the PHAC and what does it correspond to in DO-178C?**
A: The Plan for Hardware Aspects of Certification (PHAC, §10.1.1) is the hardware equivalent of the PSAC in DO-178C. It defines the proposed means of compliance with DO-254 and forms the agreement between the applicant and the certification authority on what will be done and what evidence will be produced. Like the PSAC, it must be submitted and agreed to early in the program, before full implementation is committed. Key additional content specific to PHAC: the Functional Failure Path Analysis (FFPA) for Level A/B hardware, the design assurance strategy for complex components, and the tool assessment/qualification plan.

**Q3: How does DO-254 address structural coverage compared to DO-178C?**
A: DO-178C explicitly defines structural coverage criteria (statement/decision/MC/DC/data coupling/control coupling) measured against source code. DO-254 does not mandate equivalent coverage criteria because hardware verification is fundamentally different. Instead, DO-254 uses requirements-based verification (verifying all hardware requirements are met) complemented by review and analysis. For Level A and B complex hardware (Appendix B), "Advanced Analysis Methods" are required — these can include: exhaustive simulation (for bounded input spaces), formal verification (mathematical proofs), additional functional test coverage on gate-level designs, or architectural mitigation (redundancy, monitoring, dissimilarity). The applicant must propose and obtain agreement from the certification authority on the advanced analysis strategy early in the program.

**Q4: What is an FFPA (Functional Failure Path Analysis) and why is it important for Level A hardware?**
A: A Functional Failure Path Analysis (FFPA, Appendix B §2) divides a hardware item into separate functional failure paths (FFPs) based on function, design assurance level, technology, or service history. It allows justifying different design assurance strategies for different portions of the same hardware item. For Level A hardware, the FFPA is important because: (1) it identifies which circuits are in Level A FFPs and which are in lower-level FFPs, allowing more efficient verification focus; (2) it enables architectural mitigation — if the Level A FFP contains redundant, independent, dissimilar channels, independence credit may reduce the complexity-driven verification burden on individual channels; (3) it enables justifying mixed-DAL hardware items where only portions implementing Level A functions need Level A rigor. The FFPA must be included or planned for in the PHAC.

**Q5: What are the key differences between HC1 and HC2 configuration management categories in DO-254?**
A: HC1 (Hardware Control Category 1) requires full CM activities: unique configuration identification, baseline establishment, baseline traceability, problem reporting, full change control (records, approvals, and traceability), change review, release, retrieval, data retention, protection against unauthorized changes, and media selection/refreshing/duplication. HC2 requires a subset: configuration identification, retrieval, data retention, and protection against unauthorized changes only. HC2 data is expected to be superseded rather than incrementally modified. The key practical difference: HC2 data does not need formal baselines, full change control records, or release management. Appendix A Table A-1 specifies which data items get HC1 vs HC2 at each DAL. Hardware requirements at all DALs → HC1. Review and analysis results at all DALs → HC2.
